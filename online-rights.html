The Online Safety Act 2023

This landmark law came into force in 2024 and is one of the most significant pieces of internet legislation in the world. Under the Act, platforms operating in the UK must:

  • Remove illegal content quickly — including hateful, fraudulent, or violent content
  • Enforce their own community guidelines consistently and fairly
  • Provide clear, accessible appeals processes when they remove content or suspend accounts
  • Offer extra protections for children under 18
  • Be transparent about how their recommendation algorithms work
Ofcom enforces the Online Safety Act. If a platform repeatedly fails to comply, Ofcom can fine them up to 10% of their global annual turnover — or block access to the platform in the UK entirely.

Content removal & appeals

If a platform removes your post, video, or account, you now have the legal right to a clear explanation and a fair appeals process. This applies to Instagram, TikTok, YouTube, X (Twitter), Facebook, and all major platforms operating in the UK.

  1. Read the reason given

    Platforms must explain which rule your content allegedly broke. If no reason is given, that's itself a breach of the Online Safety Act.

  2. Use the in-app appeal process

    All major platforms have an appeal option. Use it and provide context — especially if you think the removal was an automated error.

  3. Request a human review

    If the first appeal fails, explicitly request that a human moderator reviews the decision. Platforms must offer this option.

  4. Complain to Ofcom

    If the platform fails to follow a proper process, report them at ofcom.org.uk. They risk serious fines.

Account bans vs content removal: Getting a full account suspended is harder to challenge than a single content removal. Screenshot everything and download your data before you lose access.

Your GDPR data rights

UK GDPR gives you powerful rights over your personal data. These apply to any company that holds information about you — not just social media platforms, but shops, employers, banks, and more.

📋

Right of access

Request a copy of all personal data a company holds on you (a Subject Access Request).

✏️

Right to rectification

Ask them to correct inaccurate or incomplete data about you.

🗑️

Right to erasure

Ask them to delete your data — the "right to be forgotten".

⏸️

Right to restrict

Ask them to stop processing your data while a dispute is being resolved.

📦

Right to portability

Request your data in a machine-readable format to take to another service.

🚫

Right to object

Object to your data being used for direct marketing — they must stop immediately.

Subject access requests (SARs)

A Subject Access Request (SAR) forces any company to show you all personal data they hold about you. Companies must respond within one calendar month, completely free of charge.

You don't need a special form. Simply email their Data Protection Officer and say:

"I am writing to make a Subject Access Request under Article 15 of UK GDPR. Please provide all personal data you hold about me, including the purposes for which it is processed, who you share it with, and your retention period."
If they don't respond in time: Complain to the Information Commissioner's Office (ICO) at ico.org.uk — it's free and the ICO has real enforcement powers.

Right to erasure — getting your data deleted

You can ask any company to delete all personal data they hold about you. They must comply if the data is no longer needed for its original purpose, you withdraw your consent, or you object to processing and there's no overriding legitimate reason to continue.

Erasure isn't always possible. Companies can refuse if they need to keep the data to comply with a legal obligation (e.g. tax records) or for public interest reasons. They must explain why if they refuse.

Social media harassment

Online harassment is a criminal offence in the UK under several laws including the Online Safety Act 2023, the Communications Act 2003, and the Protection from Harassment Act 1997. It is illegal for someone to:

  • Send messages that are grossly offensive or threatening
  • Persistently contact you in a way that causes distress
  • Publish private images of you without consent (revenge porn)
  • Incite others to pile on and harass you

What to do if you're being harassed online

  1. Document everything first — screenshot with timestamps before blocking or reporting
  2. Report to the platform — they must act on illegal content under the Online Safety Act
  3. Report to the police — use 101 (non-emergency) or 999 if you feel at immediate risk. Online harassment is treated seriously

Defamation online

Defamation is the publication of a false statement that damages someone's reputation, covered by the Defamation Act 2013. For a statement to be defamatory it must be false, published to at least one other person, and have caused (or be likely to cause) serious harm to your reputation.

Truth is an absolute defence — you cannot sue for defamation over something that is true.

Defamation law is expensive. Cases are heard in the High Court and can cost tens of thousands of pounds. Always seek specific advice from a media law solicitor before taking action. Many offer free initial consultations.

Fake accounts impersonating you

Having someone impersonate you online is illegal under the Fraud Act 2006 and the Online Safety Act. Here's what to do:

  1. Report to the platform immediately — all major platforms have an impersonation report option and are legally required to act quickly
  2. Report to Action Fraud (actionfraud.police.uk) if money is being collected in your name
  3. Report to the police if this is part of a stalking or sustained harassment campaign
Tip: When reporting a fake account, include a link to your genuine, legitimate profile. This confirms you are the real person and speeds up the platform's review significantly.

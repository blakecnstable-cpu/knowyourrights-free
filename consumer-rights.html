The Consumer Rights Act 2015

This is the main law protecting you when you buy goods, services, or digital content in the UK. Anything you buy from a business must be:

  • Of satisfactory quality — not broken, damaged, or below the standard a reasonable person would expect
  • Fit for purpose — it must do what it's supposed to do, and what you told the seller you needed it for
  • As described — it must match the description given in-store, online, or on the packaging

If a product fails any of these three tests, you have legal remedies — regardless of what the shop's own returns policy says. A "no refunds" sign does not override your statutory rights.

Your rights exist whether or not you have a receipt. Bank statements, card statements, or a witness can prove you made a purchase. Don't let shops tell you otherwise.

The 30-day right to reject

If a product is faulty, you can return it for a full refund within 30 days of buying it. You do not have to accept a repair, replacement, or store credit — you are entitled to your money back.

Within 30 days — full refund

Right to reject and receive a complete refund. No questions asked about the fault.

30 days to 6 months — one repair or replacement

The retailer gets one chance to repair or replace. If that fails, you can demand a full refund.

6 months to 6 years — you prove the fault

The burden shifts to you. If you can show the fault was pre-existing, you may still get a repair, replacement, or partial refund.

Second-hand goods from businesses: Your rights still apply but the quality standard expected is lower, reflecting age and condition. Private sales (Facebook Marketplace etc.) have much weaker protections.

Repair, replacement & the final right to reject

After 30 days, if a fault appears within the first 6 months, the law presumes it existed at purchase. The retailer must repair or replace at no cost to you.

If the repair or replacement fails — or is impossible — you can then claim a price reduction or a final right to reject (a refund, possibly slightly reduced after 6 months to account for use).

Online shopping — extra protections

When you buy online, by phone, or from a catalogue, the Consumer Contracts Regulations 2013 give you extra rights on top of the Consumer Rights Act.

The 14-day cooling-off period

You can cancel most online purchases within 14 days of receiving them — for any reason whatsoever, even if the item is perfect. The retailer must refund you within 14 days of getting the goods back.

Exceptions: Personalised items, perishable goods, unsealed audio/video/software, and hotel or flight bookings are usually excluded from the cooling-off period.

Who pays return postage?

If the retailer's terms say you pay, you do — unless the item is faulty, in which case they must cover all return costs.

Digital content rights

Apps, games, films, music, and ebooks must also be of satisfactory quality, fit for purpose, and as described. If a download is broken or a streaming service doesn't work as advertised, you're entitled to a repair or replacement — or a price reduction if that's impossible.

Tip: If an app breaks after an update, or a downloaded game is unplayable at launch, the developer or platform is liable under the Consumer Rights Act — not just their own refund policy.

Services — builders, mechanics, hairdressers & more

Anyone you pay to do something must carry out the service with reasonable care and skill. Services must also be completed within a reasonable time and at a reasonable price if these weren't agreed upfront.

If they fail: ask them to redo the work at no cost. If they won't, get someone else to fix it and reclaim the cost. In some cases you're entitled to a price reduction.

Got scammed? Here's exactly what to do

Act immediately — speed makes a huge difference to whether you recover your money.

1

Call your bank's fraud line now

Use the number on the back of your card. If you paid by bank transfer, ask for an urgent recall.

2

Request a chargeback

If you paid by debit or credit card, ask your bank for a chargeback — it disputes the payment directly.

3

Section 75 (credit card)

Paid over £100 by credit card? Your card provider is jointly liable under the Consumer Credit Act 1974.

4

Report to Action Fraud

Report at actionfraud.police.uk or call 0300 123 2040. Get your crime reference number.

New APP scam protections (2024): If you were tricked into sending money by bank transfer, UK banks must now reimburse most victims up to £85,000 under the new PSR rules.

How to complain effectively

  1. Put it in writing — always email rather than just calling. Creates a paper trail.
  2. Name the Consumer Rights Act 2015 — it signals you know your rights and means business.
  3. Set a 14-day deadline — give them clear time to respond before escalating.
  4. Use an ombudsman — many sectors have free ombudsman services (Retail, Financial, Energy).
  5. Small Claims Court — for claims up to £10,000, use Money Claims at gov.uk. Filing costs £35–£455.
